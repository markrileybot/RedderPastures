UN plans to colonize Mars

In 2034 the UN gathered for an emergency session to address the status of Earth and its fitness to support life. The UN Secretary-General convenes an emergency session of the General Assembly to address the status of Earth and its fitness to support life now and in the future. In his opening remarks, the Secretary-General paints a grim picture of the planet's health. He cites the following alarming trends:

Rising global temperatures

Increasing extreme weather events

Declining biodiversity

Pollution of air, water, and soil

The Secretary-General warns that these trends are putting the future of life on Earth at risk. He calls on world leaders to take urgent action to address the potential consequences of inaction.

The session concludes with a call for action. The delegates agree to work together to develop a plan to address the climate crisis and protect the planet. The emergency session is a historic event. It is the first time that the UN General Assembly has convened to address the status of Earth. The session is a clear sign that world leaders are recognizing the urgency of the climate crisis. In addition to resolving to clean up the Earth another secondary resolution is promoted and accepted. The UN General Assembly votes to address the possibility that their efforts will fail and that realistically they must plan an alternative. That alternative is establishing a colony on Mars just in case, so that the human species can continue. So this two prong approach shows that world leaders are willing to take action to protect the planet and that they recognize something must be done to ensure the existence of humankind.

"The Nazca Lines are a group of geoglyphs made in the soil of the Nazca Desert in southern Peru. They were created between 500 BCE and 500 CE by people making depressions or shallow incisions in the desert floor, removing pebbles and leaving differently colored dirt exposed."
